## 1. Overview of the Problem

B University's application process had two critical failure points:

**Problem 1 — Low Engagement & High Drop-Off**
Students described the process as "boring, paperwork-heavy, and stressful."
Gen Z disengages rapidly from static, text-heavy processes and expects
personalised, interactive digital experiences (Jisc, 2020; Goh & Yang, 2021).
Prototype testing showed significant improvements in ease, enjoyment, and
completion likelihood with Beyond B vs the traditional method.

**Problem 2 — Low Decision-Making Confidence**
79% of prospective students sought external help with course selection or
personal statement writing (UCAS, 2022). This lack of confidence directly
correlates with higher dropout rates.

---

## 2. The Beyond B Solution

A behaviourally informed, gamified web platform redesigning the university
application experience for Gen Z applicants.

### Why a Web Platform (Not an App)?

| Dimension | Student Benefit | University Benefit |
|---|---|---|
| Platform Independence | Works on any device, no download needed | Broader reach |
| No Storage Required | Instant access | No app store delays |
| SEO Capability | Discoverable via search | Organic applicant reach |
| Cost & Speed | — | Cheaper to build, faster to launch |
| Maintenance | — | Easier updates and iteration |

## 4. Professional Reflection — What I Would Do Differently

### 4.1 Overestimating Gamification Appeal
**What happened:** Assumed gamification universally resonates with Gen Z
and proposed playful design elements before gathering user evidence.
Some stakeholders viewed early prototypes as unprofessional.

**Lesson:** Validate aesthetic preferences with data before presenting
design solutions. Never lead with instinct when evidence is available.

---

### 4.2 Narrowly Defining the Problem
**What happened:** Initial analysis focused on student behavioural barriers
(ambiguity, overwhelm, low confidence) but overlooked organisational
feasibility — staffing, digital maturity, integration complexity.

**Lesson:** Desirability, feasibility, and viability must be assessed
simultaneously from project initiation. Stakeholder Q&A exposed gaps
in my implementation thinking.

---

### 4.3 Prioritising Aesthetics Over Clarity
**What happened:** First Quest Map design was visually engaging but
cognitively confusing — adding to the very overwhelm the platform
aimed to reduce.

**Lesson:** Clarity must precede creativity. Apply cognitive load
principles (Huang, Hew & Lo, 2020) before aesthetic refinement.
Test logic with low-fidelity sketches first.

---

### 4.4 Inadvertent Team Dominance
**What happened:** Ability to structure arguments quickly led to
dominating early discussions, reducing contribution from quieter
members and narrowing the idea space.

**Lesson:** Use structured ideation — silent brainstorming,
round-robin sharing — and wait for all members to contribute
before synthesising or directing.

---

### 4.5 Ethics as an Afterthought
**What happened:** Initially overlooked ethical concerns including
predictive analytics bias, nudging behaviour, and unclear data
usage practices.

**Lesson:** Ethics must be embedded from project initiation.
Prioritise student autonomy, explainable analytics, and transparent
data-usage frameworks from day one.

---

### 4.6 Premature Convergence
**What happened:** Sought a definitive solution too quickly,
converging before adequate iteration and testing.

**Lesson:** Consulting requires comfort with ambiguity. Assumptions
must be tested early and iteratively — insights evolve through
discovery, not immediate resolution.

---

## 5. Collaboration Reflection

Key improvements I would make to team working:
- Establish a **team charter** at project outset
- Foster **constructive conflict** rather than consensus-seeking
- Use facilitated turn-taking to ensure equitable contribution

Diverse viewpoints directly strengthen solution defensibility —
something I initially underestimated.

| # | Insight | Application |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Data before instinct | Evidence must precede design decisions |
| 2 | Feasibility is not secondary | Operational constraints shape deliverables |
| 3 | Ethics is foundational | Not an add-on to functionality |
| 4 | Clarity over creativity | Cognitive load principles drive design |
| 5 | Iteration beats perfection | Consulting is a discovery process |

## 7. Conclusion

Beyond B demonstrated measurable potential to improve engagement,
reduce cognitive load, and support better student decision-making.
The greatest professional development came from recognising and
critically addressing my own assumptions and working practices.

These reflections will directly shape how I approach future digital
transformation consulting engagements — with greater emphasis on
evidence, ethics, collaboration, and implementation realism.
